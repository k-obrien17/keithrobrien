A CEO I'd never met sent me a draft last fall. He'd written it with ChatGPT. It was clean. Subjects and verbs agreed. The hooks landed. Every paragraph supported the last one. He wanted to know if I thought it was ready to ship.

I read it twice. I told him no.

The writing was clean. It didn't believe anything. Strip his name off, swap in any other VP of marketing at any other SaaS company, and the piece would have read the same. AI had given him 800 words of fluent prose around a premise no one would argue with.

That's the thing nobody says out loud about AI-assisted writing: AI doesn't challenge you. It accepts the thesis you gave it and decorates around it. If your thesis is "every business is going through digital transformation," AI will write you a smart-sounding piece confirming exactly that. If your thesis is wrong, AI will write a smart-sounding piece confirming a wrong thesis.

The promise of a ghostwriter worth hiring is that they push back before the words happen. They ask the two questions AI will not.

## Is that actually what you believe?

Half the time the executive doesn't know yet. The premise they handed you was a placeholder. They borrowed it from a conference talk or a board deck. A ghostwriter who accepts it writes a draft they can't stand behind a year later.

## Who cares?

This is the question that kills more LinkedIn posts than any other. Most thought leadership content is true. The problem is nobody who reads it has a strong reaction. The CEO felt smart writing it. The audience felt nothing reading it. That's a missed quarter of attention.

AI is incapable of asking either question. Models are good at fulfilling prompts. The prompt is the premise. The premise is what should be on trial. By the time AI starts writing, the trial is over.

---

## The 30-second test

There's a test you can run in 30 seconds. Take any AI-assisted executive piece. Strip the byline. Strip the company name. Read it cold. If you can substitute any other VP of marketing in the same vertical and the piece still tracks, it didn't carry a POV. It was content. It wasn't thinking.

Most B2B LinkedIn content fails this test. So do most ghostwritten bylines. The genre is dominated by hedged opinions, three-point lists nobody disagrees with, and conclusions that already existed before the writer sat down. AI compounds the problem because it makes producing this kind of safe content faster. Volume goes up. Signal goes down.

The reason this matters more in B2B than in consumer marketing is that B2B is bought on POV. Your audience is small. They read everything from the same person. Inconsistency reads as fake. The CMO they trust is the CMO who has a take that survives challenge, gets refined when challenged, and shows up the same way across a board deck, a sales call, and a LinkedIn post.

A POV that survives challenge is what makes you a CMO. Or a founder. Or worth listening to.

## What I actually do with AI

Across nine years ghostwriting at Total Emphasis for executives at IBM, McKinsey, Sodexo, Realeyes, UpWave, and other B2B companies, I've used AI every day. I use it for research, for surfacing counterarguments, for re-drafting a passage I've already pushed past the wall. I use it to find structural problems in an outline. I use it to imitate a journalist's house style so a draft lands the first time at PRWeek instead of the third.

What I don't do is let it write the thinking. That part is mine, and when I'm ghostwriting, it's my client's. The work is in the conversation that happens before the draft. The disagreement. The "wait, do you actually believe that." The hour where the executive realizes the premise they were going to publish is wrong, the new premise is what they actually think, and the new premise is harder to defend, which is exactly why it's worth publishing.

Most executives hire a ghostwriter to outsource production. The job a ghostwriter should actually do is be the editor who refuses the draft.

AI is fine at production. It is structurally incapable of being the editor who refuses the draft.

---

## What to do instead

If you're vetting someone to ghostwrite for you or your CEO, ask them what they say no to. Skip the services menu. Ask what they refuse to write. If they don't have an answer, they're production. You can buy production from a tool now.

If you're using AI to draft your own content, run the 30-second test before you ship. Strip the byline. Read it cold. If anyone else could have published it, you didn't say anything. Go back and try to write the one sentence the audience won't agree with on the first read. That's the post.

AI doesn't challenge you. That's the entire problem with shipping it raw. Writing happens in the moment someone with skin in the game decides the premise is worth defending. The typing is the easy part. That moment is still yours.
